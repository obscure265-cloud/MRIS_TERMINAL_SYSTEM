OVERVIEW

The M.R.I.S. (Morphic Resonance Integration Studies) is an organization that oversees several smaller divisions operating under its name. Its priority is to capture and study abnormal creatures that exist among the human race, as well as to discover new species or entities whether paranormal, experimental, or natural. This work is carried out with the support of associated organizations and PMCs such as M.S.V.G (Chemical Labs.), P.A.R.F (Research Facility), S.R.O.C (Private Military Company.), and the A.E.R.D (Paranormal Research.)

ENTITY CLASSIFICATIONS PANEL
> There are three types of entities in the (M.R.I.S.) System and they are:
Natural Entities
These entities exist in the same physical dimension as humans. It's unclear whether they developed at the same time as us or much earlier, but current evidence suggests they evolved naturally within our environment. Their abilities are based on normal biological and physical processes, relying on the specific structure and functions of their bodies.
Experimental Creations
These entities were created or modified in laboratory environments, usually from genetically or physically altered human subjects. It's still uncertain whether they originated from M.R.I.S. facilities or from independent, unidentified labs. Their abilities are fully biological in nature and function through normal physical processes, even though their origins are engineered.
Paranormal Entities
These entities are extremely hard to study because they fall completely outside the range of human sensory perception. They can't be seen, heard, felt, or detected, and even advanced instruments show no measurable readings from them. Their capabilities exceed known biological and physical limits, and their forms appear unrestricted, allowing them to shape into anything, icluding figures that a human can see and interact with.

The Wild Humans, also known as Human Predators, were a species spoken of in past centuries. Some claimed they lived alongside early humans, while others believed they existed long before us, but the truth remains uncertain. Few travelers ever documented them in detail, and fewer still described them accurately. However, most accounts agreed on their feral nature, their aggression, and their instinct for dominance. These beings, later classified as Homo sapiens ferus, were significantly larger and taller than the average human. This distinction set them apart, yet they were not entirely unlike us.
Both male and female variants, designated WMH (Wild Male Human) and WFH (Wild Female Human), share core human anatomical structures but exhibit extreme amplification in mass and skeletal density. Average female specimens measure between 1.9 and 2.4+ meters in height with mass ranging from 175243+ kg. Male specimens demonstrate even greater dimensions, typically exceeding 2.4+ meters and surpassing 197250+ kg. Despite significant mass accumulation, both variants maintain exceptional agility and balance, attributed to dense musculoskeletal structure and highly optimized joint morphology.
Skeletal analysis reveals extraordinary engineering. Complete WH skeletons weigh up to 55+ kg for females and 63+ kg for malesapproximately 150% above human baseline. Cranial mass alone reaches between 12.515 kg, with bone plate density capable of withstanding severe impacts.

Muscle fibers demonstrate increased myoglobin concentration and vascular density, resulting in endurance metrics far exceeding human norms. Adipose tissue comprises 3040% of total mass in females and 1525% in males, functioning as both insulation and energy reserve rather than metabolic excess. All physiological systems appear optimized for survival efficiency rather than comfort or conservation.
Cognitive assessment places WH intelligence within survival-specialized domains rather than abstract reasoning. WFH behavior demonstrates strategic environmental awareness and primitive tool use, while WMH cognition reflects problem-solving ability focused on direct adaptation. Intelligence is practical and situationalcentered on instinct, endurance, and territorial dominance rather than creativity or technology.
Social organization follows a male-led tribal hierarchy. WFH specimens typically protect smaller familial units, managing offspring and habitat maintenance. WMHs assume leadership, defense, and hunting roles within large tribes. This social structure emphasizes cooperation, discipline, and strong familial loyalty, where each member's role is vital to the tribe's survival and moral balance.
Geographic distribution indicates environmental specialization. Approximately 50% inhabit cold tundra regions, 40% occupy forests, and 10% dwell in desert plateaus (So far). Desert variants exhibit the greatest physical strength and daytime visual acuity, forest variants possess higher intelligence and social adaptability, and snowy variants display resilience to low temperatures and superior nocturnal vision.
Research indicates average lifespan ranges between 109139 years for males and 125162 years for females. The species shows remarkable longevity relative to Homo sapiens, potentially due to enhanced cellular repair mechanisms and stress-tolerant metabolic systems. Population studies record 202 female and 189 male discoveries so far, across 19 desert, 121 forest, and 251 snowy specimens.
Ongoing research continues under M.R.I.S. and MSVG supervision, with current findings categorizing their intelligence type as survival-oriented, their structure as large male-led tribes, and their habitats primarily distributed across boreal, tundra, and arid plateaus.
